Abstract Free-air CO2 enrichment (FACE) technology was used to expose a loblolly pine (Pinus taeda L.) forest to elevated atmospheric CO2 (ambient   200 µl l–1). After 4 years, basal area of pine trees was 9.2�arger in elevated than in ambient CO2 plots. During the first 3 years the growth rate of pine was stimulated by ~26�In the fourth year this stimulation declined to 23�The average net ecosystem production (NEP) in the ambient plots was 428 gC m–2 year–1, indicating that the forest was a net sink for atmospheric CO2. Gross primary production was increased by 18�The largest uncertainties in the carbon budget remain in separating belowground heterotrophic (soil microbes) and autotrophic (root) respiration.
