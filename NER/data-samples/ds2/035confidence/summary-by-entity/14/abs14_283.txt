Abstract Bank lending to small firms in the U.S. fell substantially during the recent financial crisis and ensuing recession. Because small firms account for a disproportionate share of new job creation, lending to these firms could have important implications for the pace of economic recovery. This paper examines the extent to which changes in banks’ supervisory ratings are associated with changes in the growth rate of their lending to small businesses.
